# Chapter 10: What Actually To Do

## 10.1 The Purpose of This Chapter

The preceding chapters have described how power operates—who holds it, what mechanisms maintain it, what trends are emerging. This chapter translates that analysis into action. Understanding how the world works is necessary but insufficient. You must also know what to do with that understanding.

This chapter provides a framework for personal navigation and ascent. It is not a paint-by-numbers guide—your circumstances, advantages, and goals are particular to you. But it identifies the principles that underlie effective action and provides concrete steps you can adapt.

The orientation throughout is practical, not ideological. The goal is not to change the system (though you may work toward that) but to position yourself effectively within it while the system persists. You can critique the game while playing it well. Indeed, playing well may be prerequisite to having any influence over the rules.

## 10.2 The Foundation: See Clearly

Before acting, you must see. Most people navigate using false maps provided by institutions that benefit from their confusion. They optimize for goals they were given rather than goals they chose. They work within frames that limit their options without realizing the frames exist.

**What clear seeing requires:**

**Accepting uncomfortable truths.** The monetary system transfers wealth from you to those closer to money creation. The political system is less responsive to voters than to donors. The information environment is shaped by interests that may not align with yours. The economic game is not fair. Accepting these truths is not pessimism—it's realism. Pessimism would be thinking nothing can be done. Realism is the prerequisite for doing something effective.

**Distinguishing what you're told from what you observe.** The news tells you the economy is growing; your purchasing power is declining. The politicians tell you they serve your interests; their donors' interests consistently prevail. The institutions tell you they're trustworthy; their track records suggest otherwise. When words and evidence conflict, trust evidence.

**Understanding your actual position.** Where are you in the Cantillon gradient? What assets do you own? What skills do you have? What networks do you belong to? What is your actual runway—how long could you survive without income? Most people overestimate their position because it's uncomfortable to acknowledge vulnerability. Accurate self-assessment is essential for effective action.

**Identifying your actual goals.** What do you want? Not what you're supposed to want, not what would impress others, but what you actually want when you're honest with yourself. Many people spend years pursuing goals they inherited without examination—the degree, the career, the lifestyle—only to find achievement hollow. Clarity about goals precedes effective pursuit of them.

## 10.3 Building the Base: Layers 1-4

TEOF's ten-layer hierarchy applies to individuals as to systems. Before pursuing higher layers (power, influence, meaning), you must establish lower layers (foundation, energy, network, defense). Attempting to build power without a foundation is building on sand.

**Layer 1: Unity (Coherent Identity)**

You need to know who you are independent of external validation. What are your values? What do you stand for? What will you not compromise regardless of incentive?

This is not about being rigid or moralistic. It's about having a stable core that persists across circumstances. The person whose identity shifts based on social context has no foundation. The person whose values change based on convenience cannot be trusted—by others or by themselves.

**Practical steps:**
- Articulate your values explicitly. Write them down. What matters to you?
- Identify where your stated values diverge from your revealed preferences. Where do you say one thing and do another?
- Make decisions aligned with your values even when costly. This builds integrity.
- Develop a clear sense of purpose that transcends any particular job, relationship, or circumstance.

**Layer 2: Energy (Biological and Financial Substrate)**

Your capacity to act depends on physical and financial resources. Without health, all else is compromised. Without financial runway, you're a slave to immediate necessity.

**Health:**
- Sleep: 7-9 hours, consistent schedule. Sleep deprivation degrades cognition, willpower, and health.
- Exercise: Resistance training builds strength and metabolic health. Cardiovascular training builds endurance. Both are non-negotiable.
- Nutrition: Real food, adequate protein, minimal processed inputs. You are literally built from what you eat.
- Stress management: Chronic stress degrades every system. Develop practices that provide genuine recovery.

**Finances:**
- Know your burn rate. Track spending until you know exactly what your life costs monthly.
- Build runway. Six months of expenses liquid is minimum. Twelve is better. This buffer enables saying no to bad opportunities and yes to risky good ones.
- Eliminate high-interest debt. Debt at credit card rates is an emergency. Pay it off before investing in anything else.
- Understand the difference between saving and investing. Saving is holding cash for emergencies. Investing is putting capital to work. Both are necessary, but they serve different functions.

**Layer 3: Propagation (Network Redundancy)**

Your options depend on your network. The person with diverse, strong relationships has opportunities the isolated person never sees.

**Income diversification:** Multiple income streams protect against any single source failing. The person with one job is one firing away from crisis. The person with multiple streams has resilience.

**Relationship diversification:** Friendships, professional contacts, mentors, and allies across different domains. If all your relationships are in one industry, one geography, or one community, your network fails when that domain fails.

**Skill diversification:** Competencies that transfer across contexts. The person whose skills only matter in one narrow niche is vulnerable to that niche disappearing.

**Geographic optionality:** The ability to move if necessary. This might mean maintaining relationships in multiple locations, having skills valuable across borders, or holding assets in multiple jurisdictions.

**Layer 4: Defense (Protection Against Threats)**

You need protection against the ways things can go wrong—legal, financial, informational, and physical.

**Legal structure:** Assets protected from creditors and lawsuits. This might mean LLCs, trusts, or other structures depending on your situation. Consult professionals; this is not DIY territory.

**Insurance:** Appropriate coverage for health, property, liability. Insurance is not wasted money—it's protection against catastrophic downside.

**Information security:** Protect your data, communications, and online presence. Use strong passwords, encrypted communications when appropriate, and be deliberate about what you share publicly.

**Reputation management:** What you say publicly follows you forever. Build a reputation for competence and integrity. Guard it against unnecessary controversy.

**Only with these four layers established should you focus on acquiring power and influence.** Attempting Layer 7 (Power) without Layers 1-4 is like building a tower without a foundation—impressive until the first strong wind.

## 10.4 Energy Capture: Accumulating Resources Faster Than You Expend

Power requires resources. Resources come from capturing more value than you consume. This is not complicated in principle, but requires discipline in practice.

**Human Capital: Become More Valuable**

Your ability to capture resources depends on your value to others. Value comes from solving problems, from rare skills, from reliable execution.

**Skill selection:**
- Choose skills with leverage: skills where your output scales beyond your input. Writing, coding, sales, leadership, capital allocation—each allows one person to affect outcomes far beyond what one person could produce directly.
- Choose skills that are rare and valuable: common skills command low prices. Rare skills command premiums. The intersection of rare and valuable is where you want to position.
- Choose skills that compound: some skills become more valuable with experience. Others become obsolete. Prefer skills that improve with practice and don't age out.
- Choose skills that transfer: skills valuable in only one context leave you vulnerable to that context disappearing. Skills valuable across industries and geographies provide resilience.

**The highest-leverage skills in the current environment:**

**Code:** Software is eating the world. The ability to build software creates disproportionate impact. Even non-engineers benefit from understanding what software can do.

**Media/Communication:** The ability to capture attention and communicate effectively is valuable everywhere. Writing, speaking, creating content that resonates—these skills have unlimited upside in an attention economy.

**Sales:** Everything gets sold. Products, services, ideas, yourself. The person who can sell has options the person who cannot does not. Sales skills transfer across every domain.

**Leadership:** The ability to coordinate people toward a goal multiplies your impact. Organizations pay premiums for people who can lead effectively.

**Capital allocation:** Knowing where to put money—your own or others'—to generate returns. This skill scales infinitely; a good decision about a million dollars takes the same cognitive effort as a good decision about a thousand.

**Financial Capital: Compound Your Resources**

**The formula is simple:** Spend less than you earn. Invest the difference. Let time do the work.

**Spend less than you earn:** Your savings rate is more important than your income in determining wealth accumulation. Someone earning $100K and saving 30% accumulates faster than someone earning $200K and saving 5%.

**Invest the difference:** Money sitting in a bank account is losing value to inflation. Money invested in productive assets—equities, real estate, businesses—compounds over time.

**Let time do the work:** Compound returns are the engine of wealth creation. The earlier you start, the more powerful the compounding. A dollar invested at 22 does more work than a dollar invested at 42.

**Where to invest:**

**Index funds:** Low-cost exposure to the broad market. This is the default allocation for capital you don't need short-term. Don't try to beat the market unless you have edge; most people who try lose to the index.

**Real estate:** Tangible asset with multiple return streams: appreciation, rent, tax benefits, leverage. Housing you live in is not primarily an investment; rental property is.

**Your own business:** The highest returns are available to entrepreneurs who build successful businesses. This is also the highest risk. Appropriate for capital you can afford to lose, inappropriate for your emergency fund.

**Skill development:** Investing in yourself often has the highest returns. A course that makes you 10% more productive might be worth more than any financial investment.

**What to avoid:**

**Speculation without edge:** Trading, crypto, options, complicated strategies. Unless you have genuine edge—information or skill that others lack—you're gambling with sophisticated counterparties who do have edge.

**Lifestyle inflation:** As income rises, spending tends to rise with it. Each raise gets absorbed into a slightly more expensive lifestyle, leaving savings rate unchanged. Deliberately decouple spending from income.

**Depreciating assets on credit:** Cars, consumer goods, experiences financed with debt. These purchases cost more than their sticker price and build no wealth.

**Social Capital: Build Genuine Relationships**

Your network determines your options. Opportunities come through people. Information comes through people. Resources come through people.

**Provide value first:** Relationships are not transactions, but they are reciprocal. People help those who have helped them. Build a reputation as someone who gives value before asking for anything.

**Be useful:** Do you have skills others need? Do you make introductions? Do you share information that helps people? Being useful attracts people who are themselves useful.

**Show up consistently:** Relationships require maintenance. The friend you don't see for five years is not really a friend. Regular contact, even brief, maintains connection.

**Diversify domains:** If all your relationships are in one industry, your network is fragile. Build connections across industries, geographies, and social contexts.

**Cultivate depth, not just breadth:** Thousands of LinkedIn connections are worth less than ten people who would take your call at midnight. Prioritize deep relationships over wide ones.

**Attention Capital: Own Distribution**

In an attention economy, owning an audience is power. The person who can reach thousands or millions of people has leverage the person who cannot does not.

**Build a platform:** Write, speak, create content, build an audience. Even modest followings open doors that credentials alone do not.

**Own your distribution:** Email lists, owned websites, direct relationships. Platform followers can be taken away (through algorithm changes or bans); owned distribution cannot.

**Become known for something:** Generic presence is invisible. Be known as the person who knows about X—whatever X is most valuable for you to own.

**Demonstrate judgment:** Track record matters. Make predictions, share analysis, show your work. Over time, correct judgment builds reputation more than any credential.

## 10.5 The Paths to Power

With a foundation established and resources accumulating, there are multiple paths to power. Different paths suit different people. Choose based on your advantages and goals.

**Path 1: Wealth Accumulation**

Build or acquire cash-flowing assets. Compound wealth over decades. Convert wealth to influence when needed.

**Timeline:** 20-40 years for substantial wealth. Faster with entrepreneurial success, slower with pure accumulation.

**Advantages:** Flexible, doesn't require permission, compounds automatically, can be preserved across generations.

**Disadvantages:** Slow, requires patience, doesn't necessarily convert to other forms of power.

**Who it suits:** Patient, disciplined, entrepreneurial or high-saving-rate individuals. People who prefer independence over institutional position.

**Path 2: Institutional Ascent**

Enter elite institutions early. Navigate internal politics. Accumulate formal positions. Convert position to resources and influence.

**Timeline:** 20-30 years to reach senior positions. Faster with talent and luck, slower without.

**Advantages:** Leverage institutional resources, legitimate authority, network access, relatively predictable path.

**Disadvantages:** Requires conformity, success depends on institutional approval, vulnerable to institutional decline.

**Who it suits:** Socially intelligent, patient, able to work within constraints. People who value stability and legitimacy over independence.

**Entry points:** Elite universities, top-tier professional firms, government positions, established corporations.

**Path 3: Network Centrality**

Become an indispensable connector. Broker information and relationships. Build coalitions across institutions.

**Timeline:** 10-20 years to become significantly central.

**Advantages:** Information access, flexibility, influence without formal position, resilient to institutional changes.

**Disadvantages:** Requires exceptional social skills, difficult to monetize directly, depends on maintaining relationships.

**Who it suits:** Highly social, curious, willing to help others without immediate return. People who enjoy connecting people and ideas.

**Path 4: Information Leverage**

Build audience and distribution. Become authoritative in a domain. Convert attention to other forms of power.

**Timeline:** 5-15 years to build significant audience.

**Advantages:** Scalable, creates options, can build independently, compounding returns on content.

**Disadvantages:** Requires public presence (with associated risks), platform-dependent, can be attacked.

**Who it suits:** Good communicators, those with valuable perspectives, people willing to be public figures.

**Path 5: Technical Leverage**

Build infrastructure others depend on. Create platforms, standards, or tools that become essential.

**Timeline:** 5-20 years depending on domain and success.

**Advantages:** Creates durable power, can scale dramatically, less dependent on personal presence.

**Disadvantages:** Requires technical ability, success is binary (works or doesn't), intense competition.

**Who it suits:** Technical builders, entrepreneurs, those who prefer creating to persuading.

**Combining paths:** These paths are not mutually exclusive. Wealth can fund institution-building. Networks can enable information leverage. Technical success can create wealth. The most powerful positions often combine multiple paths.

## 10.6 What Behaviors Are Rewarded

Observing what behaviors are actually rewarded—rather than what behaviors are claimed to be rewarded—reveals how to succeed within the current system.

**Demonstrated competence:** Actually being good at something matters. Despite cynicism about meritocracy, ability does correlate with advancement. The gap is between "ability alone is sufficient" (false) and "ability doesn't matter" (also false). Ability is necessary but not sufficient.

**Legibility:** Credentials, affiliations, and signals that make your competence visible to those who don't know you personally. In a world where most people assess you superficially, legible signals matter. Elite university degrees, prestigious employers, recognized achievements—these are not just vanity; they're sorting mechanisms that open doors.

**Reliability:** Doing what you say you will do. Many people are unreliable—they miss deadlines, over-promise, disappear when things get hard. Simply being reliable differentiates you from a large portion of the population.

**Political skill:** Reading social situations, managing relationships, avoiding unnecessary enemies. Technical ability matters less if you alienate everyone you work with. The ability to navigate organizations and relationships multiplies the impact of other abilities.

**Shamelessness:** This is uncomfortable but true. Many successful people succeed partly through willingness to self-promote, to ask for things, to take credit, to be visible. Excessive modesty is a disadvantage in competitive environments.

**Risk tolerance calibrated to situation:** Willingness to take calculated risks when upside exceeds downside. Many opportunities go to those willing to say yes when others hedge. But this must be calculated risk, not recklessness.

**Staying in the game:** Not blowing up. Avoiding catastrophic failures that eliminate you from play. Many careers are destroyed not by mediocrity but by a single catastrophic mistake. Consistent good performance beats alternating brilliance and disaster.

**Pattern recognition:** Seeing trends before they're obvious, positioning accordingly. Those who see shifts coming can position in advance; those who don't are surprised and disadvantaged.

**Narrative skill:** Constructing compelling stories about yourself, your work, your organization. Facts matter less than narrative in many contexts. The person who can frame their work compellingly has advantage over the person who does equal work but describes it poorly.

## 10.7 What Behaviors Are Punished

Equally important is understanding what behaviors are punished, so you can avoid unnecessary friction.

**Visible dissent from core consensus:** Some beliefs are sacred—questioning them creates career risk. Identifying which beliefs are sacred in your environment and avoiding visible dissent unless you're willing to pay the price. This is not about your private views; it's about what you say publicly.

**Making powerful people look bad:** Even if you're right, even if it's important, even if they deserve it—embarrassing powerful people creates enemies with resources. Choose battles wisely.

**Unreliability:** Missing commitments, being late, dropping balls. This destroys trust faster than almost anything else.

**Visible desperation:** Needing the job, needing the deal, needing the approval. Desperation signals weakness and attracts exploitation. Even when you do need something, project that you don't.

**Burning bridges:** The world is smaller than it seems. People you alienate will reappear. Industries are more interconnected than they look. Treating people well even when you think you don't need them is self-interested as well as ethical.

**Excessive controversy without proportional benefit:** Being publicly controversial when you don't have the resources to survive the consequences. High-profile dissent can be rewarding if you have audience or institutional protection; it's destructive if you don't.

**Ignoring implicit rules:** Every environment has explicit rules and implicit rules. The implicit rules are often more important. Learning what is actually expected, not just what is officially stated, is essential for navigation.

## 10.8 Strategic Positioning

Beyond tactical behaviors, think about strategic positioning—where you place yourself for compounding advantages.

**Position for asymmetric upside:** Seek situations where downside is bounded but upside is unbounded. Early-stage companies, emerging fields, situations where a bet can pay off hugely if it works and fail cheaply if it doesn't.

**Position where value flows:** Money flows along predictable channels. Regulatory changes create and destroy value in predictable patterns. Position yourself where value is flowing toward, not away from.

**Position for optionality:** Build situations that give you multiple paths forward. The person with one option is captive; the person with multiple options can choose.

**Position as node, not leaf:** In networks, some positions are central and some are peripheral. Central positions provide information access and influence. Position yourself where connections flow through you, not where you're at the end of a chain.

**Position for the next cycle:** Economic and political conditions are cyclical. Position for where things are going, not where they've been. This requires pattern recognition and willingness to act before consensus confirms.

## 10.9 Ethical Navigation

How do you navigate a system that may require compromise while maintaining integrity?

**Distinguish compliance from endorsement:** You can comply with rules you don't endorse. Paying taxes doesn't mean you approve of how they're spent. Working within institutions doesn't mean you accept their legitimacy. Compliance is tactical; endorsement is belief.

**Identify your non-negotiables:** What will you not do regardless of incentive? Know before you're tested. When the moment comes, you won't have time to think clearly.

**Preserve private integrity:** You may need to say things publicly you don't fully believe. You should not lie to yourself. The person who knows they're playing a game maintains integrity the person who forgets it's a game does not.

**Build toward independence:** The more resources you accumulate, the more you can afford to act according to your values rather than necessity. Financial independence enables ethical independence.

**Recognize that complicity has degrees:** Working within a system involves some level of complicity in that system's effects. But there are degrees. You can work within a system while minimizing harm, building alternatives, and positioning to change it.

**Play positive-sum games:** Where possible, succeed through value creation rather than value extraction. Extraction creates enemies and depletes the environment you depend on. Creation builds allies and improves conditions for future rounds.

## 10.10 Scenario Preparation

The future is uncertain. Rather than betting everything on one scenario, position to survive and thrive across multiple possibilities.

**If current trends continue:** Increasing wealth inequality, technological change, institutional decay. Position: own assets, develop skills, build network, maintain optionality.

**If inflation accelerates:** Currency debasement, asset price increases, wage-price spirals. Position: own real assets, have debt at fixed rates, have income with pricing power.

**If deflation/depression occurs:** Asset price collapse, credit contraction, unemployment. Position: have cash reserves, no fragile debt, skills valuable in hard times.

**If political instability increases:** Institutional breakdown, social conflict, policy unpredictability. Position: geographic optionality, community ties, self-reliance capabilities.

**If technological disruption accelerates:** AI displacement, industry destruction and creation. Position: skills that augment technology rather than compete with it, equity exposure to transformative companies.

**If your particular sector declines:** Your industry may be disrupted regardless of macro trends. Position: transferable skills, network in multiple industries, financial cushion.

No one position is optimal for all scenarios. The goal is resilience—being positioned to adapt rather than being optimized for a single future that may not arrive.

## 10.11 Immediate Actions

If this chapter provides useful reframes, translate them into immediate action. Knowledge without application is worthless.

**This week:**
- Calculate your actual net worth and monthly burn rate
- Identify your largest financial vulnerabilities
- Assess your skill market value—what would you earn if you had to find a new position tomorrow?

**This month:**
- Begin tracking spending if you don't already
- Identify one skill to develop that would increase your value
- Make one new professional connection
- Assess your insurance coverage and legal protections

**This quarter:**
- Increase savings rate by at least 5 percentage points
- Make concrete progress on skill development
- Build relationship with at least one mentor or advisor
- Create or update estate documents

**This year:**
- Build runway to 6 months expenses if not already there
- Develop one additional income stream, however small
- Substantially advance in chosen skill area
- Expand network meaningfully in a new domain
- Make one investment in an asset class you haven't used before

**Ongoing:**
- Continue all of the above—this is not a one-time exercise
- Regularly audit your position against this framework
- Adjust based on changing circumstances
- Compound advantages over time

## 10.12 The Long Game

The strategies described here operate over years and decades, not weeks and months. This is uncomfortable in a culture of instant gratification. But it is reality.

Wealth compounds. Skills compound. Relationships compound. Reputation compounds. Those who think in decades accumulate advantages that those who think in quarters cannot match.

The person who starts at 22, saves consistently, develops skills systematically, builds relationships genuinely, and makes good decisions most of the time will, by 45, have options that the person who drifts will not. Those options compound further through the back half of life.

The game is long. Play accordingly.
