## Money and Banking

### Foundational Sources

**"Money Creation in the Modern Economy" (Bank of England, 2014)**
- Quarterly Bulletin article by McLeay, Radia, and Thomas
- Definitively establishes that banks create money through lending
- Not controversial theory—official central bank explanation
- Available free at bankofengland.co.uk

**"The Federal Reserve System: Purposes and Functions" (Federal Reserve)**
- The Fed's own explanation of its structure and operations
- Available at federalreserve.gov
- Primary source for how the Fed describes itself

### Books on Money

**"The Creature from Jekyll Island" by G. Edward Griffin**
- Critical history of Federal Reserve creation
- Perspective: Libertarian, anti-Fed
- Strengths: Detailed historical narrative
- Weaknesses: Sometimes veers into conspiracy territory; check specific claims
- *Read critically but contains useful historical material*

**"Lords of Finance" by Liaquat Ahamed**
- Pulitzer Prize-winning history of central banks between WWI and Great Depression
- Perspective: Mainstream, balanced
- Strengths: Excellent historical narrative, well-sourced
- *Highly recommended for understanding central banking history*

**"The Deficit Myth" by Stephanie Kelton**
- Modern Monetary Theory (MMT) perspective
- Perspective: Progressive, pro-government spending
- Strengths: Clear explanation of how government finance actually works
- Weaknesses: Policy prescriptions are contested
- *Useful for understanding government money creation, not endorsement of MMT politics*

**"When Money Dies" by Adam Fergusson**
- History of Weimar hyperinflation
- Perspective: Historical, cautionary
- Primary source for hyperinflation dynamics
- *Essential reading on monetary collapse*

### Papers and Reports

**"Testing Theories of American Politics" by Gilens & Page (2014)**
- Academic study finding elite preferences predict policy, public preferences don't
- Perspectives on Politics journal
- Foundational source for oligarchy claims
- *Methodologically sound; conclusions are contested but study is robust*

## Power and Governance

### Books

**"The Power Elite" by C. Wright Mills (1956)**
- Classic sociological analysis of American power concentration
- Perspective: Critical/left
- Dated but foundational; the analysis structure remains relevant
- *Read for framework, update examples*

**"Dark Money" by Jane Mayer**
- Investigation of Koch network and conservative donor influence
- Perspective: Liberal, critical of right
- Well-documented but one-sided; equivalent left networks less examined
- *Read alongside coverage of liberal donor networks for balance*

**"Winners Take All" by Anand Giridharadas**
- Critique of philanthropy and "doing well by doing good"
- Perspective: Progressive
- Well-argued critique of elite problem-solving
- *Useful for understanding elite self-conception*

**"Capital in the Twenty-First Century" by Thomas Piketty**
- Comprehensive analysis of wealth concentration
- Perspective: Social democratic
- Data-rich; policy conclusions contested
- *Essential data source on wealth inequality*

**"Manufacturing Consent" by Edward Herman & Noam Chomsky**
- Media criticism from propaganda model perspective
- Perspective: Left
- Influential model of media function
- Pre-internet but framework still applicable
- *Read for model, recognize limitations*

**"Propaganda" by Edward Bernays (1928)**
- Primary source from the founder of public relations
- Perspective: Practitioner (not critical)
- Reveals the explicit thinking behind mass persuasion
- *Short, essential primary source*

## Economics and Markets

### Books

**"The Intelligent Investor" by Benjamin Graham**
- Classic value investing text
- Perspective: Practical, long-term oriented
- Warren Buffett's primary influence
- *Foundational for investment thinking*

**"A Random Walk Down Wall Street" by Burton Malkiel**
- Case for index investing
- Perspective: Academic/efficient markets
- Well-argued for passive investing
- *Good for most investors*

**"Antifragile" by Nassim Nicholas Taleb**
- Framework for thriving in uncertainty
- Perspective: Iconoclastic, probability-focused
- Sometimes arrogant but genuinely insightful
- *Essential for thinking about risk and optionality*

**"The Bitcoin Standard" by Saifedean Ammous**
- Economic case for Bitcoin
- Perspective: Austrian economics, pro-Bitcoin
- Best articulation of Bitcoin maximalist position
- *Read critically; it's advocacy, not neutral analysis*

**"The Price of Tomorrow" by Jeff Booth**
- Deflationary thesis on technology
- Perspective: Tech entrepreneur, pro-Bitcoin
- Interesting argument about technology and money
- *Speculative but thought-provoking*

## On Critical Reading

### How to Approach These Sources

**No source is neutral.** Every author has perspective, incentives, and blind spots. The goal is not to find the "unbiased" source but to triangulate across sources with different biases.

**Check primary sources when possible.** Secondary sources (books, journalism) summarize and interpret. When claims matter, trace to primary sources (documents, data, studies).

**Consider the author's incentives.** Who benefits if the argument is accepted? This doesn't determine truth but contextualizes claims.

**Track predictions.** Authors who made accurate predictions in the past have earned more credence than those who were wrong. Past accuracy is the best indicator of future reliability.

**Beware of confirmation bias.** We're drawn to sources that confirm existing beliefs. Actively seek sources that challenge your views.

### Sources This Book May Underweight

In the interest of intellectual honesty, here are perspectives this book may underweight:

**Mainstream economics:** The book is skeptical of mainstream economic institutions but mainstream economics has genuine insights about markets, trade, and growth.

**Progressive institutionalism:** The book criticizes institutions but some progressive critiques focus on reforming rather than rejecting institutions.

**Optimistic futurism:** The book tends toward cautionary perspectives on technology and social change; genuine optimists make cases worth considering.

**Conventional financial advice:** Index investing and conventional portfolio theory have strong track records; unconventional views should clear a high bar.
