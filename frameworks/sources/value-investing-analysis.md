# Value Investing Analysis

**Domain:** Capital allocation, investment, wealth preservation
**Track Record:** ~100 years (Graham since 1920s), 60+ years (Buffett track record)
**Observable Outcomes:** 19.9% CAGR over 60 years (Buffett), multiple billionaire practitioners

---

## Why It Persists

Value investing has survived nearly a century while countless other investment strategies have come and gone:

1. **Grounded in math, not narrative** - Intrinsic value is calculable
2. **Contrarian by nature** - Buys fear, sells greed (survives market cycles)
3. **Margin of safety** - Built-in error protection
4. **Long-term orientation** - Avoids short-term noise
5. **Teachable** - Clear principles that can be transmitted (Graham → Buffett → next generation)

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Intrinsic Value

Every security has an intrinsic value "justified by facts (assets, earnings, dividends and prospects)." The market price fluctuates around this value but eventually converges to it.

**Why it works:**
- Separates price (what you pay) from value (what you get)
- Creates objective anchor for decision-making
- Ignores market sentiment, focuses on fundamentals

### 2. Margin of Safety

The difference between intrinsic value and market price. Buy only when price is significantly below value.

**Why it works:**
- Protects against calculation errors
- Cushions against unforeseen events
- Limits downside while preserving upside
- "The lower the price, the higher the Margin of Safety"

### 3. Mr. Market

View the market as a manic-depressive partner who offers to buy/sell at prices reflecting his mood, not reality.

**Why it works:**
- Reframes market volatility as opportunity, not threat
- Creates psychological distance from daily fluctuations
- Enables buying when others panic, selling when others euphoric

### 4. Circle of Competence

Only invest in businesses you understand deeply.

**Why it works:**
- Reduces errors from ignorance
- Enables genuine value assessment
- Buffett famously avoided tech for decades—still outperformed

### 5. Long-Term Holding

"I buy on the assumption that they could close the market the next day and not reopen it for five years." - Buffett

**Why it works:**
- Lets compounding work
- Reduces transaction costs and taxes
- Avoids timing mistakes
- Forces focus on business quality, not price movements

### Benjamin Graham

- Back-tested his approach for 50+ years
- Students became "Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville"

## Core vs Peripheral

**Core (stable since 1920s):**
- Intrinsic value exists and is calculable
- Buy below value (margin of safety)
- Mr. Market is irrational short-term
- Stay within circle of competence
- Hold for long term
- Diversify for safety (Graham: 40+ stocks)

**Peripheral (adapts to context):**
- Specific valuation metrics (P/E, P/B ratios change with interest rates/era)
- Sector focus (Graham avoided tech, some value investors now include it)
- Concentration vs diversification (Buffett more concentrated than Graham)
- Active vs passive implementation

---

## Selection Pressures Survived

1. **Great Depression** - Graham-Newman profitable throughout
2. **Multiple recessions** - Value investors historically outperform in downturns
3. **Tech bubbles** - Avoided dot-com crash by staying in circle of competence
4. **Efficient Market Hypothesis** - Academic attack on premise, practitioners continued outperforming
5. **Growth/momentum dominance** - 2010s were "worst decade for value," still positive returns
6. **Quantitative competition** - Algorithms haven't eliminated value opportunities

## Observable Outcomes

**Practitioners demonstrate:**
- Long-term wealth accumulation (multiple billionaires)
- Lower drawdowns in market crashes
- Psychological equanimity during volatility
- Ability to act when others freeze (buying 2008-2009)
- Compound returns over decades

**Non-practitioners lack:**
- Objective framework for assessing opportunity
- Psychological tools for handling volatility
- Discipline to wait for right prices
- Compounding over long periods (frequent trading destroys returns)

## What to Borrow

1. **Margin of safety principle** - In all decisions, build in cushion for error
2. **Separate price from value** - What something costs vs what it's worth (applies beyond investing)
3. **Mr. Market mindset** - Treat external noise as opportunity, not signal
4. **Circle of competence** - Only operate where you understand deeply
5. **Long-term orientation** - Let compounding work, avoid short-term optimization
6. **Contrarian patience** - Best opportunities when others panic

---

## What to Leave

1. **Specific metrics** - P/E < 15, P/B < 1.5 were Graham's era; principles matter, not numbers
2. **Stock-picking requirement** - Index investing is valid for those not doing deep analysis
3. **40-stock diversification** - Buffett concentrated more; depends on conviction and skill
4. **Ignoring qualitative factors** - Buffett evolved beyond pure Graham to include business quality

---

## Graham's 7 Criteria (For Reference)

1. Adequate size (large companies)
2. Strong financial condition (current ratio > 1.5)
3. Earnings stability (positive EPS last 5 years)
4. Dividend record (consistent payouts)
5. Earnings growth (higher than 5 years ago)
6. Moderate P/E ratio (< 15)
7. Moderate P/B ratio (< 1.5, or P/E × P/B < 22.5)

These are specific implementations of core principles, not the principles themselves.

## TEOF Connection

Value investing is Pattern C applied to capital allocation:
- **Core:** Intrinsic value, margin of safety, long-term, circle of competence (unchanged 100 years)
- **Operational:** Valuation methods, sector selection, position sizing (adapts per era)
- **Tactical:** Individual stock picks, timing (changes constantly)

The approach persists because it's **grounded in reality** (what businesses actually earn) rather than **narrative** (what people hope prices will do). This is observation primacy applied to investing.
